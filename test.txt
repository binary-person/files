      <div class="wikiDescription" ng-non-bindable>Primitive animals have body plans that are very different from ours. Animals such as jellyfish, corals and flatworms have a much simpler way to handle the digestion of food, as well as the circulation of nutrients around the body. Learn about the structure and function of the gastrovascular cavity in these organisms.</div>
   
	
	<div id="articleMain" class="wikiContent faded-content" >

		<h2 id="section---WhatIsAGastrovascularCavity">What Is a Gastrovascular Cavity?</h2><p>The <b>gastrovascular cavity</b> is a structure found in primitive animal phyla. It is responsible for both the digestion of food and the transport of nutrients throughout the body. The cavity has only one opening to the environment. Food goes in and waste comes out that same opening, making it a <b>two-way digestive tract.</b> </p><p>By contrast, organisms that have a mouth on one end with an anus on the other end have a one-way digestive tract, called an <b>alimentary canal.</b> Food goes in the mouth, while waste comes out the anus. </p><p></p><table border="0" class="imageplugin"> 
 <caption align="bottom">
  A gastrovascular cavity (left) has only one opening, while an alimentary canal (right) has two.
 </caption> 
 <tbody>
  <tr>

  </tr> 
 </tbody>
</table><p></p><h2 id="section---AnimalPhyla">Animal Phyla</h2><p>There are two phyla under Kingdom Animalia that possess a gastrovascular cavity. The first is <b>Cnidaria</b>, which includes jellyfish, corals, hydras and sea anemones. Cnidarians show radial symmetry, which means that they are similar when transected across the middle. The second is <b>Platyhelminthes</b>, which includes flatworms, such as Planaria, and tapeworms. Platyhelminthes show bilateral symmetry, which means they have identical halves when transected longitudinally. </p><p></p><table border="0" class="imageplugin"> 
 <caption align="bottom">
   Radial symmetry (left) vs. bilateral symmetry . Each line segment divides the organism into roughly equal halves. Note that in bilateral symmetry, only one such division is possible.
 </caption> 
 <tbody>
  <tr>
   <td><img src="/cimages/multimages/16/Symmetry.png" alt="Symmetry" /></td>
  </tr> 
 </tbody>
</table><p></p><h2 id="section---BasicStructureAndFunction">Basic Structure and Function</h2><p>The gastrovascular cavity is essentially an empty space in the organism that is surrounded by tissue. Food is brought into the cavity. The tissue surrounding the cavity secretes enzymes to enable digestion. Nutrients are then absorbed directly by the cells surrounding the cavity and distributed to the rest of the organism through diffusion. The organisms possessing a gastrovascular cavity are very simple and made of only a few tissues. Therefore, there is no need for a true circulatory system. </p><h2 id="section---Cnidaria">Cnidaria</h2><p>The gastrovascular cavity in Cnidaria is surrounded by an inner tissue layer called the <b>gastrodermis.</b> The outside of the organism has a tissue layer called the <b>epidermis.</b> Between these two tissue layers is a gel-like region called the <b>mesoglea.</b> </p><p>The mesoglea is made of mostly water with some fibrous tissue. It serves as a sort endoskeleton for the organism. Phagocytic cells called <b>amoebocytes</b> wander through the mesoglea, devouring invading organisms. In some of the more advanced cnidarians, the mesoglea is more developed, having muscle and nerve tissue. </p><p>It should be noted that cnidarians come in two general body plans - the medusa form found in jellyfish, and the polyp form found in corals, hydras and sea anemones. </p><p></p><table border="0" class="imageplugin">

The thin nature of the bell or vase, consisting of only the gastrodermis, mesoglea and epidermis, allows for sufficient respiration by diffusion. </p>

			<div class="hidden" ng-non-bindable><h2 id="section---Platyhelminthes">Platyhelminthes</h2><p>Flatworms do not have the vase-like cavity structure of cnidarians. Instead, the cavity is more of a branching structure that runs the length of the entire body. The opening to the cavity is called the <b>pharynx</b>. Food enters and waste leaves through the pharynx, which in some species extends through the mouth as a tube. </p><p>The gastrovascular cavity branches thoroughly enough to serve the nutritional needs of the entire organism through diffusion. Like cnidarians, flatworms have an inner tissue layer called the <b>endodermis</b> that lines the gastrovascular cavity, as well as an outer layer called the epidermis. Between these is the mesenchyme, which contains muscles and nerves. </p><p>The flat structure of Platyhelminthes allows for easy diffusion of gases between the organism and the environment. Other structures in flatworms include eye spots for detecting light, nerves that sometimes converge to form primitive brains and reproductive organs. </p><p></p><table border="0" class="imageplugin">

</table><p></p><h2 id="section---LessonSummary">Lesson Summary</h2><p><b>Gastrovascular cavities</b> represent the first evolutionary step in serving the nutritional and circulatory needs of animals with tissues. They rely upon diffusion and the thinness of the organisms' tissue layers to allow the spread of nutrients throughout the organism. </p><p><b>Cnidarians</b> have cup-like or vase-like forms. Their gastrovascular cavities conform to this general shape. <b>Platyhelminthes</b> specimens have relatively complex, branching cavities, as well as flat shapes to help the diffusion of gases between the organisms and their environments. </p><h2 id="section---KeyTerms">Key Terms</h2><p></p><table border="0" class="imageplugin"> 
 <tbody>
